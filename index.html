01 · Partner Experience AuditReading the advisor's actual experience

The homepage is fine. The FAQ tells the real story.

I read Ren's advisor resources the way an advisor actually would, not as marketing copy. One finding mattered more than the rest.

Highest-priority finding

The Giving Season FAQ works as an incident log, not as advisor enablement.

1Observation

The FAQ bundles a dozen real advisor questions into one page, published right as Giving Season volume peaks.

2Evidence

Live on the page today: which portal to log into, why an account isn't showing online yet, when credentials arrive, whether the December 19 deadline applies.

3Business impact

Each unanswered question becomes a support ticket and a delayed grant, in the exact window where processing speed decides whether a gift counts for the year's deduction.

4Recommendation

Replace the reactive FAQ with a tiered advisor calendar that answers these questions before October. Full brief in section 04.

Already working

The custody-neutral pitch answers the firm's biggest fear, losing the client relationship, directly on the homepage. Rare directness for enterprise software. Worth leaning on harder, not changing.

Gap to flag

Messaging is built for firms with no DAF program yet. Firms running a legacy or in-house program get no explicit switch case, even though that's a meaningful slice of the realistic pipeline.

04 · Sample Campaign BriefGiving Season Advisor Activation

Closing the gap the audit found, before November

The brief that turns the Giving Season FAQ into a planned, tiered advisor activation, September through year-end.

🎯Objective

  • Primary: Cut Giving Season support volume tied to login and account-status confusion by moving advisor education earlier in the calendar
  • Secondary: Increase advisors introducing DAFs before Q4, when processing is fastest and decisions are calmer
  • Business signal: Earlier funding, which Ren's own advisor resources already flag as the better outcome

👥Audience tiers

  • Tier 1, Catalysts Club: high-engagement, gets the calendar first so they can model it for peers
  • Tier 2, occasional: recommended a DAF once or twice, needs a reminder and a leave-behind, not a re-pitch
  • Tier 3, never-recommended: needs the basic case, tied to a real scenario like a stock sale or a high-income year

✍️Key messages by tier

  • Tier 1: "Clients who fund early get faster processing. Here's this week's one-pager."
  • Tier 2: "It's been a while. Here's the 90-second version for your next review."
  • Tier 3: "Clients who discuss giving with their advisor are far more likely to donate appreciated assets instead of cash. That conversation is the value-add."

🤖Where AI actually does the work

  • Cluster last year's Giving Season support tickets with an LLM to surface the 3 or 4 recurring objections before October, instead of relearning them live
  • Summarize Catalysts Club relationship manager notes into monthly theme briefs, so Tier 1 messaging reflects what advisors are actually asking
  • Flag advisors with no DAF recommendation in 12 months from existing platform data, and route them into Tier 3 automatically
Calendar: September through year-end

05 · Competitive LandscapeRen isn't competing with Fidelity Charitable for the same decision

The real choice a firm is making

Fidelity Charitable and Schwab Charitable (now DAFgiving360) are the names every DAF conversation starts with, and they're enormous: Fidelity alone granted $14.9 billion in 2024. For a wealth management firm, though, that's not the actual comparison on the table.

DimensionRen-powered programFidelity Charitable / Schwab CharitableBuild an in-house program
Whose brand the client sees The firm's own name on the program, Ren stays invisible behind it A competing wealth manager's brand, often the exact firm trying to retain that client The firm's own name, but every workflow behind it has to be built from scratch
Advisor's role after launch Unchanged: custody and investment-neutral, advisor keeps managing the relationship Often diminished: the client's charitable assets sit with someone else's investment team Unchanged, but the advisor inherits new administrative and compliance load too
Time to launch Turnkey program available through Renaissance Charitable, or a dedicated branded instance Not applicable, there's no firm-branded version to launch Months to years, plus ongoing compliance and tax administration overhead
Grant disbursement Rapid Disbursement Engine: over half of grants approved instantly, ACH payout Mature, high-volume disbursement at consumer scale Dependent on whatever the firm builds or contracts separately
Charity network 130,000+ charities reached annually, automatic vetting once a charity is validated Large, well-established consumer-facing network Starts from zero, vetting has to be built or bought

For a wirehouse, the real competitive question was never "Ren versus Fidelity Charitable." It's whether the firm keeps that client's giving under its own name, or watches them quietly open an account somewhere else the next time the subject of charitable stock comes up.
